Do We Even Exist?

A Deep Dive into the Strange Mystery of Being Human

June 11, 2025 6 min read

What Are We?

What are we? No, seriously—what are we? Flesh? Dust? Code? Some cosmic joke? Every day we wake up, blink twice, and get pulled into this machine-world of sound, color, movement, thoughts. We look at our hands and just assume they’re real. But if you sit long enough, without distraction, that certainty starts to wobble.

Do we even exist?

We walk through cities built from concrete, glass, wires, and software. We type into glowing rectangles and speak into plastic shells. Things respond. Lights change. Programs run. Entire systems move in silence behind every tap. But who made all this? How? How did humans go from caves and sticks to satellites and AI? How does a human mind even know something will work before they build it?

How do we know anything?

You might be holding a phone right now. Swipe. Scroll. Read. Behind that glass is a matrix of microscopic metal pathways, firing electricity in pulses too fast for you to feel. But it knows. The machine reacts. A human built this. A person. Not a god. Not an alien. Some person with a heart and a brain and fears and childhood memories. Somehow, they figured out that this piece of metal, this wire, that code—if arranged just right—would make what you're holding.

But How?

At some point, someone looked at the stars and thought, I wonder if I can talk to another mind through invisible waves. And now we call that Wi-Fi. We live in a time where invisible ideas shape physical reality. We’re surrounded by proof of human belief turning into actual things. Planes fly because someone once believed metal could float on air.

What kind of creature even thinks like that?

The world isn’t just a place anymore. It’s a mind. A web of shared hallucinations we all agreed on. Countries. Time. Rules. Codes. Brands. Apps. All of it built from thought. Not from earth or bone, but from belief. Someone once sat alone, staring into nothing, and dreamed up what you’re now holding in your hand.

That should feel impossible. And yet it happens. Again and again. We ask: how do these things work? But behind every working thing is a deeper, more terrifying question—why does it work at all? Why does code turn into images? Why do spoken words trigger emotion? Why do we feel love? Hate? Emptiness? How does a signal from a screen make your chest hurt, or your eyes tear up?

This is more than science. This is myth.

You could say “because of neurons,” or “because of logic gates,” or “because of design.” But zoom in further and it all collapses. Down to particles that don’t behave. Down to a quantum foam that’s more probability than matter. Everything is shimmering uncertainty pretending to be solid. Even your thoughts aren’t really yours. They just appear. Uninvited.

  • You didn’t choose your first language.
  • You didn’t pick your parents.
  • You didn’t decide to be born.

    • And yet here you are, in a body you didn’t make, in a world you didn’t build, looking at machines you don’t fully understand, still pretending it all makes sense. Maybe that’s the deepest magic of humanity—not understanding, but acting like we do.

    Because maybe none of it really makes sense. Maybe we’re standing on a spinning rock in cold space, building glass castles in a digital sky to distract ourselves from the fact that we don’t know why we’re here.

    Maybe we’re just echoes of something deeper. Maybe consciousness is just the light coming through a crack in the cave wall. Or maybe, just maybe, we’re the universe watching itself in the mirror for the first time—confused, fascinated, and trying not to blink. Because if we blink too long, we might remember: None of this had to be. And yet, it is. And that… is the strangest thing of all.

    But it doesn’t stop there.

    Even stranger than “it is” is the fact that we care. That we cry when things go wrong. That we laugh even when nothing is funny. That we paint and write and build things that don’t feed us, clothe us, or save us—but somehow matter more than food or shelter ever could. Why do we care about meaning, when we could’ve just eaten, survived, died?

    Think about music. Just waves. Vibrations in the air. No substance, no form. But when arranged right, they move us to tears. How can a sound—just a sound—feel like heartbreak? How can lines of text on a page make your chest ache with the memory of someone you lost? These are not features of survival. These are features of something else entirely.

    What are we really?

    We are not just machines. If we were, we’d never write poetry. No algorithm needs a song. No neuron requires a painting. But here we are—bleeding onto canvases, whispering our souls into microphones, pouring our pain into code and color and rhythm.

    We are haunted by the question: What is all this for?

    And still, we build. We build like it means something. Cities. Sculptures. Languages. Dreams.

    We build gods out of pixels.

    We build futures out of blueprints.

    We build ourselves out of stories.

    But stories break too. There are nights when everything crumbles—when the tech stops, the light fades, and all you're left with is a mirror and a question: Who am I when nothing is working? Not the phone. Not the plan. Not even the body. And if you sit with that silence long enough, a strange thought appears:

  • Maybe existence isn’t about understanding.
  • Maybe it’s about witnessing.

    • Because despite all our questions, despite all the terror of not knowing—we do see. We do hear. We do feel. We touch the skin of the world with trembling hands and call it “real.” Maybe that’s all we need. You ask: how do humans make all this? How do they know it’ll work? The honest answer? They don’t. But they try anyway. They guess. They fail. They risk. They dream. And something about that—about the trying—feels more real than any proof ever could. So maybe we exist not because we think—but because we try. We dream and build and love and break and cry and rebuild. And through it all, the world keeps whispering, You are here. You are here. You are here. Even if nothing makes sense, even if the machines stop, even if everything you know falls apart— You are still here. And maybe that’s enough. Maybe that’s the only truth that matters.

    So... Do we even exist?

    Yes. And somehow, that’s both the simplest and most impossible thing of all.
